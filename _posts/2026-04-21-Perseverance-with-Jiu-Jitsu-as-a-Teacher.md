When you show up to your first Brazilian Jiu Jitsu (BJJ) class, you don't even know how to roll, literally. From your first front roll to your first "roll" (lingo for sparring), you don't know where to put your hands, your feet, your anything, and you just lay there like a deflated pancake.

There's something invaluable about that helplessness to show you that you don't know everything which allows you to have the humility to listen and learn. That humility opens up the door to perseverance. When you aren't comparing yourself to anyone, you then are competing with yourself alone. Every class you show up, even if you get submitted (absolutely choked or a joint twisted into some ungodly pose) over and over, the sport just sinks into your bones.

Slowly, you learn how to frame (how to place your arms and legs to make space between you and your opponent), so then you're at least a competent dummy. Next, you learn how to escape, and even if you have no idea what to do next, you can keep surviving for a while. Eventually, you'll finally submit someone, and then proceed to be destroyed for the rest of the day as a reward. Day after day, even through practices that feel useless, it becomes part of who you are, not just something where you go through the motions.

I find research to be much the same.

You start out wide-eyed in a brand new world with no map, and you don't even know how to read a paper, much less present your own. You go home after your first day on a new project, lay on the ground, and stare into the void while you think, *What have I gotten myself into?* In that moment, we must embrace humility and remember that every great discovery was once an unknown variable.

Each day, I'm competing against me of yesterday. Slowly, I learn the acronyms of this algorithm and that framework, so then I can at least nod and smile but mean it. Next, I learn to read and find my own papers, so I can learn to hold myself up and think on my own. One day, I have a halfway original thought, just to be critiqued to the ends of the earth by my advisor for my prize. Even in areas where I do have expertise, there is always more to learn, and like Socrates found: *the wisest of us is the one who knows they know truly nothing at all*.
